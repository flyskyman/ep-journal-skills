BACKGROUND: Catheter ablation is an established treatment for symptomatic atrial fibrillation (AF) that has failed antiarrhythmic drug therapy. Early postoperative cognitive dysfunction (POCD) has been reported after the procedure; however, whether this cognitive decline persists over the long term has not been defined, leaving the question of lasting cognitive harm unresolved.

OBJECTIVES: This study sought to determine whether AF catheter ablation is associated with persistent cognitive dysfunction at 12-month follow-up.

METHODS: In a prospective randomized controlled trial, 100 patients with symptomatic AF who had failed at least 1 antiarrhythmic drug were randomized to AF catheter ablation or ongoing medical therapy and followed for 12 months; 96 patients completed the protocol (mean age 59 +/- 12 years; 32% women; 46% persistent AF). Cognitive performance was assessed with a battery of 6 cognitive tests at baseline and at 3, 6, and 12 months. New cognitive dysfunction was compared between arms at each time point, and predictors of POCD were evaluated.

RESULTS: New cognitive dysfunction was more frequent in the ablation arm than in the medical arm at 3 months (14% vs 2%; P = 0.03), but this difference resolved by 6 months (4% vs 2%; P = NS) and was absent at 12 months (0% vs 2%; P = NS). Ablation time was an independent predictor of POCD (P = 0.03). At 12 months, significant improvement in cognitive scores was observed in 14% of ablation-arm patients versus 0% of medical-arm patients (P = 0.007).

CONCLUSIONS: POCD occurs after AF catheter ablation but is transient, with complete recovery by 12 months and no evidence of persistent long-term cognitive dysfunction; a subset of ablated patients even demonstrated cognitive improvement. These findings indicate that concern over lasting cognitive harm should not deter AF ablation in appropriately selected patients.
