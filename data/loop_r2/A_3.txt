BACKGROUND: Left atrial appendage closure (LAAC) is an established alternative to anticoagulation for stroke prevention in atrial fibrillation, and intracardiac echocardiography (ICE) is increasingly used for procedural guidance. However, patients with persistent LAA thrombus have largely been excluded from LAAC, the few reported cases have relied on transesophageal echocardiography (TEE) for guidance, and whether ICE can safely guide LAAC in the specific setting of LAA thrombus is unknown.

OBJECTIVE: To assess the feasibility and periprocedural safety of a modified ICE-guided LAAC procedure in patients with LAA thrombus.

METHODS: In this single-arm observational feasibility study, 12 consecutive patients with persistent LAA thrombus despite anticoagulation (n=10) or contraindications to anticoagulation (n=2) underwent ICE-guided LAAC with the LAmbre device between June 2021 and April 2023. Thrombus was located at the appendage apex (n=6), body (n=3), or ostium (n=3). A cerebral protection device was used in 11 patients. Periprocedural events and follow-up clinical outcomes, including TEE assessment for device-related thrombus and peridevice leak, were recorded.

RESULTS: The LAmbre device was successfully implanted under ICE guidance in 100% of patients (12/12). No thrombotic material was retrieved by the cerebral protection device in any of the 11 protected patients, and no severe periprocedural complications occurred. All patients completed TEE follow-up, with no device-related thrombus and no peridevice leak >3 mm detected. Over a median follow-up of 147 days (IQR 80-306), there were no strokes or transient ischemic attacks, no systemic embolism, and no major bleeding events.

CONCLUSION: In this small feasibility series, ICE-guided LAAC with the LAmbre device was technically successful and free of severe periprocedural complications in patients with LAA thrombus, suggesting it may be a feasible option when performed by experienced operators. These hypothesis-generating findings require confirmation in larger comparative studies before routine adoption.
