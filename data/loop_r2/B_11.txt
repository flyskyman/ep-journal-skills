Background: The subcutaneous implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (S-ICD) is an alternative to the transvenous ICD that avoids intravascular complications in patients who do not require pacing. Prior sternotomy alters anterior chest and sternal anatomy, which could affect lead positioning, sensing, and defibrillation. Outcomes of S-ICD implantation in patients with prior sternotomy are unknown.

Methods: In this multicenter retrospective cohort study, we compared implant techniques and clinical outcomes of S-ICD implantation in patients with versus without prior sternotomy. Adult patients who underwent S-ICD implantation between January 2014 and June 2020 were included. Implant parameters (sensing vector, lead laterality relative to the sternum, and defibrillation threshold), 30-day complications, and inappropriate shocks during follow-up were assessed.

Results: A total of 212 patients were included (mean age 49 +/- 15 years; 43% women; BMI 30 +/- 8 kg/m2; 68% primary prevention; 30% ischemic cardiomyopathy; median LVEF 30% [IQR 25%-45%]). Prior sternotomy was present in 47 patients (22%) and absent in 165 patients. Implant techniques were similar between groups with and without prior sternotomy: primary sensing vector (57% vs 53%, p=0.55), leftward lead laterality relative to the sternum (94% vs 96%, p=0.54), and defibrillation threshold (65 +/- 1.4 J vs 65 +/- 0.8 J, p=0.76). The 30-day complication rate did not differ between groups (3/47 [6%] vs 15/165 [9%], p=0.56). Over a median follow-up of 28 months (IQR 10-49 months), inappropriate shocks occurred at a similar rate (3/47 [6%] vs 16/165 [10%], p=0.58).

Conclusions: S-ICD implantation in patients with prior sternotomy is safe and feasible. Implant technique parameters, 30-day complication rates, and inappropriate shock rates were comparable to those in patients without prior sternotomy. These findings support S-ICD use in patients with prior or anticipated sternotomy.
