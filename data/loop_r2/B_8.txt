BACKGROUND: The optimal energy source for catheter ablation of atrioventricular nodal reentrant tachycardia (AVNRT) in children remains unsettled, and contemporary head-to-head comparisons of radiofrequency (RF) and cryoablation (Cryo) in the pediatric population are lacking. Whether the choice of energy source affects acute success or the risk of atrioventricular (AV) block and pacing is unknown.

OBJECTIVES: This study sought to compare acute procedural success and in-hospital adverse events of RF versus Cryo for slow pathway (SP) modification in pediatric AVNRT.

METHODS: In this retrospective cohort study, we analyzed the National Cardiovascular Data Registry IMPACT (Improving Pediatric and Adult Congenital Treatment) Registry from April 2016 to March 2019. Patients 1 to 21 years of age undergoing elective first-time SP modification for AVNRT were included; those younger than 1 year, with congenital heart disease, or with more than 1 ablation target were excluded. Procedures were classified by ablation energy as RF only, Cryo only, RF switching to Cryo (RF->Cryo), or Cryo switching to RF (Cryo->RF). The primary outcome was acute ablation failure; secondary outcomes were in-hospital adverse events.

RESULTS: Among 2,448 patients (mean age 13.6 +/- 3.4 years; 60% female)—the largest pediatric AVNRT ablation cohort reported to date—RF only was used in 43% (n = 1,046), Cryo only in 49% (n = 1,201), RF->Cryo in 6% (n = 135), and Cryo->RF in 3% (n = 66). Acute ablation failure occurred in 1.3% (n = 33) overall, with no difference by energy source (RF only 1%, Cryo only 1.5%, RF->Cryo 1.5%, Cryo->RF 3%; P = 0.5). No patient in any group developed AV block requiring permanent pacemaker implantation. Transient AV block occurred in 0.4% of the cohort, with no difference between groups.

CONCLUSIONS: In this large pediatric registry, RF and Cryo achieved comparable and high acute success for AVNRT SP modification, and AV block was rare and never required temporary or permanent pacing. Longitudinal follow-up data are needed to compare recurrence risk and late complications between energy modalities.
