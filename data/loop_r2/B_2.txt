BACKGROUND: Distinguishing nearfield from farfield electrograms is essential for accurate mapping and ablation of arrhythmias and for confirming pulmonary vein isolation (PVI). Current assessment relies on the subjective perception of electrogram "sharpness," and no quantitative metric exists. We hypothesized that electrogram peak frequency (PF) provides an objective measure of signal proximity that can separate nearfield from farfield signals and confirm PVI intraprocedurally.

METHODS: In this prospective cohort study, left atrial and pulmonary vein PF maps were generated before and after PVI in 30 patients with atrial fibrillation (AF) undergoing ablation. Patients were divided into 3 cohorts of 10. Cohort 1 (first 10 paroxysmal AF) was used to derive a PF cutoff distinguishing farfield from nearfield electrograms; cohort 2 (10 paroxysmal AF) and cohort 3 (10 persistent AF) were used for validation. Diagnostic accuracy of the derived cutoff was assessed across all cohorts.

RESULTS: PF was consistently lower in farfield than in nearfield electrograms. A PF cutoff of 300 Hz best differentiated farfield from nearfield signals. Sensitivity was 93.2% (95% CI, 81.3-98.6) in cohort 1, 90.0% (95% CI, 76.3-97.2) in cohort 2, and 98.6% (95% CI, 90.1-99.7) in cohort 3. Specificity was 100.0% (95% CI, 98.2-100.0). Across all patients, the area under the ROC curve was 0.99 (95% CI, 0.97-1.00).

CONCLUSION: Electrogram peak frequency reliably distinguishes nearfield from farfield electrograms and improves recognition of pulmonary vein isolation. PF-guided mapping and ablation strategies warrant further investigation to improve ablation outcomes.
