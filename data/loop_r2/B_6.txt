Background: Patients with atrial fibrillation (AF) who experience stroke or systemic embolism (SSE) without an identifiable vascular cause despite oral anticoagulant therapy (OAT), or who develop left atrial appendage thrombus (LAAT), are considered to have "malignant" left atrial appendage (LAA). The optimal strategy to reduce SSE risk in these patients is unknown, and real-world management across European centres has not been characterized. We aimed to map contemporary diagnostic and therapeutic pathways for malignant LAA.

Methods: An 18-item online questionnaire focused on the diagnosis and management of malignant LAA was disseminated by the European Heart Rhythm Association (EHRA) Scientific Initiatives Committee to physicians practising in European cardiac centres. The survey was cross-sectional and descriptive, asking respondents to report their practice and to rank preferred diagnostic tools and sequential treatment steps.

Results: A total of 196 physicians participated. Respondents reported high confidence in transoesophageal echocardiography (TEE) for the diagnosis of LAAT. For first-line treatment of malignant LAA, switching to another direct oral anticoagulant (DOAC) was the most preferred step, followed in ranked order by switching to a vitamin K antagonist (VKA), low-molecular-weight heparin, or continuing/optimizing the existing DOAC dose. LAA closure (LAAC) ranked as the last option overall. LAAC was nonetheless considered viable in patients with embolic stroke despite OAT and no thrombus on TEE ("empty LAA") after a comprehensive workup had excluded other embolic sources. In practice, the majority of physicians managed these patients as outpatients, predominantly by shifting VKA to DOAC or one DOAC to another, and reserved LAAC for patients at higher bleeding risk or with complications of malignant LAA such as stroke.

Conclusion: This survey provides a snapshot of contemporary European management of malignant LAA. Drug-switching, particularly DOAC-to-DOAC, is the dominant first-line approach and is mostly delivered on an outpatient basis, while LAAC remains a last-resort option reserved for patients with high bleeding risk or complicated disease. These findings highlight the absence of consensus and the need for prospective evidence to guide management of this challenging population.
