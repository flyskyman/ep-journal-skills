BACKGROUND: The subcutaneous implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (S-ICD) is an alternative to the transvenous ICD that avoids intravascular complications in patients without a pacing indication. However, prior sternotomy alters the anterior chest and sternal anatomy in ways that could affect lead positioning, sensing, and defibrillation, and outcomes of S-ICD implantation in this population remain unknown.

OBJECTIVE: To compare S-ICD implant techniques and outcomes between patients with and without prior sternotomy.

METHODS: In this multicenter retrospective cohort study, adult patients who underwent S-ICD implantation between January 2014 and June 2020 were included. Implant technique (sensing vector, lead laterality relative to the sternum, and defibrillation threshold) and outcomes (30-day complications and inappropriate shocks during follow-up) were compared between patients with prior sternotomy and those without.

RESULTS: A total of 212 patients were studied (mean age 49 +/- 15 years; 43% women; BMI 30 +/- 8 kg/m2; 68% primary prevention; 30% ischemic cardiomyopathy; median LVEF 30%, IQR 25%-45%), of whom 47 (22%) had a prior sternotomy and 165 did not. Implant parameters were similar between the sternotomy and no-sternotomy groups: use of the primary sensing vector (57% vs 53%, p=0.55), leftward lead laterality relative to the sternum (94% vs 96%, p=0.54), and defibrillation threshold (65 +/- 1.4 J vs 65 +/- 0.8 J, p=0.76). The 30-day complication rate did not differ (3/47 [6%] vs 15/165 [9%], p=0.56). Over a median follow-up of 28 months (IQR 10-49 months), inappropriate shocks were similar between groups (3/47 [6%] vs 16/165 [10%], p=0.58).

CONCLUSION: In this multicenter cohort, S-ICD implantation in patients with prior sternotomy was feasible and safe, with implant technique parameters, 30-day complication rates, and inappropriate shock rates comparable to those in patients without prior sternotomy. Prior sternotomy should not preclude consideration of an S-ICD in otherwise eligible patients.
