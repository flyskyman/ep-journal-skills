Background: Patients undergoing atrial fibrillation (AF) catheter ablation frequently require repeat procedures targeting pulmonary vein (PV) re-isolation and/or extra-PV substrate. Pulsed field ablation (PFA) is tissue-selective and may limit collateral damage, but the feasibility and efficacy of redo ablation using a pentaspline PFA system have not been established.

Methods: We conducted a prospective, multicenter study across 3 international centers enrolling 117 patients undergoing redo ablation with a pentaspline PFA system. Patients were stratified by presenting rhythm into 3 workflow groups: Group 1 (sinus rhythm, n=64), Group 2 (atrial flutter/atrial tachycardia, n=18), and Group 3 (AF, n=35). Propensity score matching was used to compare PFA-based with radiofrequency (RF)-based redo ablations.

Results: PV re-isolation was required in 71.9% of Group 1 and 72.2% of Group 2 patients. PFA terminated 100% of non-cavotricuspid isthmus (CTI)-dependent flutter cases and 45.7% of AF cases. One major complication occurred (0.9%; frontal cerebral hematoma). Freedom from atrial tachyarrhythmias at 12 months was 78.3% (95% CI 69.6-84.8%), with no statistically significant difference among groups (Group 1, 85.7%; Group 2, 77.0%; Group 3, 65.5%; p=0.053). Compared with matched RF-based redo procedures, PFA achieved similar arrhythmia freedom but with significantly shorter procedural and dwelling times.

Conclusions: Repeat ablation with a pentaspline PFA system was feasible, safe, and effective at 1-year follow-up. PFA provided clinical efficacy comparable to RF for redo procedures while significantly reducing procedural duration.
