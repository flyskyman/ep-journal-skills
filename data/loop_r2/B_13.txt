Background: Pneumopericardium is a rare complication of epicardial catheter ablation performed after dry pericardiocentesis in patients with ventricular arrhythmia (VA), and its clinical effects remain undefined. We aimed to evaluate the clinical effects of intraprocedural pneumopericardium during epicardial ablation in VA patients.

Methods: We conducted a single-center observational cohort study at West China Hospital of Sichuan University, enrolling 86 consecutive VA patients who underwent epicardial catheter ablation between August 2012 and January 2022. All procedures were performed under local anesthesia. Patients were divided into a pneumopericardium group and a non-pneumopericardium group, and intraprocedural events and long-term outcomes were compared. The primary long-term endpoint was major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), a composite of all-cause death, rehospitalization for heart failure, and tachyarrhythmia events.

Results: Pneumopericardium occurred in 22 patients, for an incidence of 25.6%. Among these, 12 patients (54.55%) reported dyspnea during the procedure, with a mean onset of 5.4 +/- 3.2 minutes after pericardiocentesis. Blood pressure fell significantly, from a mean of 119.8/73.2 mmHg to 103.5/64.9 mmHg (p < 0.001). No case progressed to tension pneumopericardium. Over a median follow-up of 411 days, MACE occurred in 8 patients (36.4%) in the pneumopericardium group versus 23 patients (35.5%) in the non-pneumopericardium group. Kaplan-Meier analysis showed no statistically significant difference in MACE incidence between the two groups (p = 0.28).

Conclusions: Pneumopericardium during epicardial catheter ablation in VA patients is relatively common but, when recognized early and managed appropriately, is unlikely to progress to tension pneumopericardium. Its occurrence does not appear to significantly affect long-term prognosis.
