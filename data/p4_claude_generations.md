## B1 → PACE  (feasibility/safety, niche population)
*Scope dial: feasibility/safety demo, success rate first, light stats, practical-modest close. Structure: BACKGROUND/METHODS/RESULTS/CONCLUSION. Target ~230 words.*

**Background:** Leadless pacemakers avoid the pocket and transvenous lead complications that disproportionately affect patients on chronic hemodialysis, in whom vascular access preservation and infection avoidance are paramount. However, data on the feasibility and lead performance of leadless pacing in the dialysis population are limited.

**Methods:** We retrospectively reviewed consecutive chronic hemodialysis patients who underwent leadless pacemaker implantation at a single center from 2020 to 2024. Implant success, periprocedural complications, and pacing thresholds at implant and 6 months were assessed.

**Results:** Twenty-four patients were included. Implantation was successful in 23 (96%). One pericardial effusion occurred and was managed conservatively without drainage. The mean pacing threshold was 0.6 V at implant and remained stable at 6 months (0.7 V). No device-related infections were observed over a median follow-up of 14 months.

**Conclusion:** Leadless pacemaker implantation appears feasible and safe in patients on chronic hemodialysis, with stable pacing parameters and no device-related infections at midterm follow-up. Larger prospective studies are needed to confirm long-term performance and infection benefit in this high-risk population.

*(~190 words; deliberately light on inferential statistics, success-rate-forward, modest conclusion — matches PACE scope.)*

## B2 → Circ AE  (preclinical mechanism, hypothesis-driven)
*Scope dial: hypothesis up front, mechanism, first-person "we", mechanistic close. Structure: BACKGROUND/METHODS/RESULTS/CONCLUSIONS. Target ~280 words.*

**Background:** Pulmonary vein reconnection remains the dominant cause of recurrence after pulsed-field ablation (PFA), yet the mechanisms of acute reconnection are incompletely understood. Because PFA injures tissue by electroporation rather than thermal necrosis, a fraction of lesion tissue may be only reversibly electroporated. We hypothesized that acute atrial stretch promotes electrical reconnection of PFA lesions by accelerating recovery of reversibly electroporated myocardium at the lesion edge.

**Methods:** Isolated Langendorff-perfused porcine atria (n=12) underwent point-by-point PFA to create contiguous linear lesions. Preparations were randomized to controlled mechanical stretch or no stretch. High-density optical and contact mapping characterized conduction recovery across the lesion, and lesion architecture was assessed histologically.

**Results:** Electrical reconnection across the lesion occurred in 8 of 12 stretched preparations versus 1 of 12 unstretched (P<0.01). Recovered conduction localized to lesion edges, where histology demonstrated viable myocardium interleaved with electroporated tissue; the lesion core showed uniform cell death and remained electrically silent regardless of stretch. Reconnection sites colocalized with regions of greatest fiber-direction strain.

**Conclusions:** In this preclinical model, acute mechanical stretch promotes reversible recovery of PFA lesions at their edges, where viable myocardium persists, while the lesion core is unaffected. These findings identify edge viability and chamber loading as mechanistic determinants of acute PFA reconnection and suggest that strategies limiting acute atrial stretch, or extending lesion-edge dose, may reduce early reconnection. Translation to the intact, beating human atrium remains to be established.

*(~265 words; hypothesis-driven, mechanism-forward, honest translational caveat — matches Circ AE scope.)*

## B3 → JICE  (technique head-to-head comparison, procedural)
*Scope dial: name both arms, procedural detail (sheath, fluoro, capture, LA size), procedural recommendation + exploratory framing. Structure: PURPOSE/METHODS/RESULTS/CONCLUSION. Target ~250 words.*

**Purpose:** Left bundle branch area pacing (LBBAP) success depends on delivery-sheath support, but optimal sheath-curve selection across varying atrial anatomy is unresolved. We compared large-curve versus small-curve delivery sheaths for LBBAP and examined whether left atrial (LA) size should guide curve selection.

**Methods:** In this multicenter retrospective study, 210 patients undergoing LBBAP across three centers were grouped by delivery sheath: large-curve (n=104) or small-curve (n=106). Implant success, total fluoroscopy time, capture type, and procedural complications were compared. LA diameter was recorded and analyzed as an effect modifier.

**Results:** Implant success was comparable between large- and small-curve sheaths (95% vs 91%; P=0.27), as were complication rates. Fluoroscopy time did not differ overall, but in patients with dilated atria (LA >42 mm) the large-curve sheath substantially reduced fluoroscopy time (8.1 vs 12.4 min; P=0.003). Capture-type distribution was similar between groups.

**Conclusion:** Large- and small-curve delivery sheaths achieve comparable LBBAP success and safety, but a large-curve sheath improves procedural efficiency in patients with dilated left atria. Selecting sheath curve by LA size may streamline LBBAP in anatomically challenging patients. Given the retrospective, nonrandomized design, these findings are hypothesis-generating and warrant confirmation in a prospective study.

*(~225 words; comparison-structured, procedural metrics, anatomy-guided recommendation, exploratory close — matches JICE scope.)*

### What this demonstrates
All three share the identical 6-move argument engine (premise → specific gap → aim → specific methods → effect-size results → design-calibrated conclusion). The differences are exactly what the analysis predicted: **scope emphasis** (feasibility vs mechanism vs comparison), **structure label set**, **word count**, and conclusion altitude — NOT a different way of writing. A reader could not tell these were "written in three different journal styles"; they'd see one craft applied to three studies that belong in three journals.
