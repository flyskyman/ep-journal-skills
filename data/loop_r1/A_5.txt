AIMS: Smartphone photoplethysmography (PPG) can detect atrial fibrillation (AF), yet current guidelines do not endorse it for diagnosis without confirmatory ECG. Prior validation was performed supervised in healthcare settings, and atrial flutter (AFL) was rarely assessed, so unsupervised ambulatory performance remains uncharacterised. We aimed to validate unsupervised, patient-operated smartphone-PPG against simultaneous single-lead ECG for AF and AFL rhythm diagnosis at home.

METHODS AND RESULTS: In this prospective diagnostic validation study, 280 unselected patients undergoing direct-current cardioversion for AF or AFL performed 1-min heart-rhythm recordings at home, at least twice daily for 30 days. The CORAI Heart Monitor PPG app (iPhone 7) was run simultaneously with single-lead ECG (KardiaMobile) as reference, yielding 18 005 paired recordings. PPG and ECG were read independently by two experienced readers. PPG recordings reached sufficient diagnostic quality more often than ECG (96.9% vs. 95.1%; P < 0.001). Against manually interpreted ECG, manual PPG reading achieved a sensitivity of 97.7%, specificity of 99.4%, and overall accuracy of 98.9% when AFL recordings were included. Excluding AFL recordings, sensitivity was 99.0%, specificity 99.7%, and accuracy 99.5%.

CONCLUSION: A novel smartphone-PPG method, used unsupervised by patients at home, provides accurate AF and AFL rhythm diagnosis with very high sensitivity and specificity and recording quality at least equal to single-lead ECG. These findings support smartphone-PPG as a standalone heart-rhythm diagnostic device after cardioversion, without requiring ECG confirmation.
