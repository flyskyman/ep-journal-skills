Background: In symptomatic Brugada syndrome (BrS), the prognostic value of the arrhythmogenic substrate extent remains poorly defined, and the long-term safety and efficacy of epicardial radiofrequency ablation (RFA) compared with implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD) therapy alone have not been validated. We aimed to assess the prognostic impact of substrate size and to validate the long-term outcomes of epicardial RFA versus no-RFA.

Methods: In this prospective investigational long-term registry study, 257 selected symptomatic BrS patients all received an ICD. Of these, 206 underwent epicardial RFA and were monitored for more than 5 years post-ablation (RFA group), whereas 51 declined ablation and received an ICD only (no-RFA group). Arrhythmogenic substrates were localized on the epicardial surface of the right ventricle, with ajmaline used to guide epicardial mapping and ablation. We evaluated risk factors for ventricular fibrillation (VF) events before ablation and freedom from VF events after ablation.

Results: During pre-ablation follow-up (median 27 months), 53 patients experienced VF episodes or VF storms. On multivariable analysis, independent risk factors for VF events were substrate size (hazard ratio [HR] 1.13, 95% CI 1.08-1.18; P<0.001), aborted cardiac arrest (HR 2.98, 95% CI 1.68-5.28; P<0.001), and SCN5A variants (HR 2.22, 95% CI 1.15-4.27; P=0.017). During post-ablation follow-up (median 40 months), the RFA group had significantly superior freedom from VF events compared with the no-RFA group (P<0.001), with no major procedure-related complications.

Conclusion: The extent of the arrhythmogenic substrate is a crucial prognostic factor for recurrent VF in symptomatic BrS. Ajmaline-guided epicardial RFA is safe and effective compared with ICD therapy alone over long-term follow-up. These findings support future exploration of non-invasive methods to guide therapeutic decision-making in BrS.
