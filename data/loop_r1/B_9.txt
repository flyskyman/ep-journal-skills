Background: Guidelines for electrophysiology study (EPS) and catheter ablation in children with Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome are primarily age-based. However, body size may be a more physiologically relevant determinant of procedural risk and success, and the relationship between weight and ablation outcomes in pediatric WPW remains poorly defined.

Objectives: This study sought to determine whether body weight is associated with major adverse events (MAEs) and acute ablation success in children undergoing first-time EPS for WPW.

Methods: We performed a multicenter retrospective cohort study using the IMPACT (Improving Pediatric and Adult Congenital Treatment) registry of the National Cardiovascular Data Registry, including patients enrolled at 84 centers from April 2016 to December 2019. Children aged 1 to 21 years with WPW undergoing first-time EPS were included; those with congenital heart disease, cardiomyopathy, or more than 1 ablation target were excluded. The cohort was dichotomized at a weight threshold of 30 kg (1 SD below the cohort mean) into <30 kg and >=30 kg groups. The primary outcome was MAEs; additional outcomes included deferred ablation, cryoablation use, and acute ablation success.

Results: Among 4,456 subjects, 14% weighed <30 kg. Compared with children >=30 kg, those <30 kg more often had preprocedural supraventricular tachycardia (45% vs 29%; P<0.001) and less often had right septal accessory pathways (25% vs 33%; P<0.001). MAEs were rare but more frequent in the <30 kg group (0.3% vs 0.05%; P=0.04). Rates of deferred ablation (9% vs 12%; P=0.07) and cryoablation use (11% vs 11%; P=0.70) did not differ. Acute ablation success was higher in the <30 kg group (95% vs 92%; P=0.009), an association that persisted after multivariable adjustment for covariates including accessory pathway type and location (odds ratio: 1.6; 95% CI: 1.01-2.70; P=0.046).

Conclusions: In children undergoing first-time EPS for WPW, weight <30 kg was associated with a small but significant increase in MAEs, with similar rates of deferred ablation and cryoablation across weight groups. After adjustment, weight <30 kg was an independent predictor of acute ablation success. These findings support incorporating body size, in addition to age, into procedural risk-benefit assessment for pediatric WPW ablation.
