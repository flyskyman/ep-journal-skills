Background: Cardioneuroablation (CNA) targets ganglionated plexi (GPs) to modulate cardiac autonomic tone in patients with functional atrioventricular (AV) block and cardioinhibitory vasovagal syncope. The optimal strategy for mapping and localizing GP and autonomic targets remains undefined. In particular, it is unclear how fractionated-area mapping compares with peak-frequency-area mapping and how peak frequency varies across cardiac regions.

Methods: We performed a single-center retrospective analysis of 10 patients undergoing CNA (cardioinhibitory vasovagal syncope, n=7; functional AV block, n=3) using high-density electroanatomical mapping. Two mapping approaches were compared—fractionated area mapping and peak frequency area mapping (threshold >550 Hz)—each assessed across 22 predefined cardiac regions.

Results: The median fractionated area was 0.8 cm2 (IQR 0.3–1.6) and the median area of regions with peak frequency >550 Hz was 0.6 cm2 (IQR 0.3–1.0), with no significant difference between approaches (p=0.152). The median peak frequency at the left superior GP, where asystolic or bradycardic responses occurred, was 623.0 Hz (IQR 582.0–668.0), versus 602.0 Hz (IQR 582.0–668.0) in regions with tachycardic responses (p=0.738). Ablation sites overlapped both mapping areas in 7 of 10 patients, whereas 2 patients had ablation sites confined to peak-frequency areas only. Peak frequency values were highest in the septal and posterior atrial walls, the superior vena cava, and GP regions, indicating heightened autonomic activity in these zones.

Conclusions: Fractionated-area mapping and peak-frequency (>550 Hz) mapping provide complementary rather than interchangeable information on CNA targets, and regional variation in peak frequency reflects underlying autonomic heterogeneity. These findings support integrating multiple mapping modalities to improve procedural precision and outcomes in CNA.
