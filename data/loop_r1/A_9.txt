BACKGROUND: Guidelines for electrophysiology study (EPS) and catheter ablation in Wolff-Parkinson-White (WPW) syndrome are stratified primarily by age. However, body size may be a more direct determinant of procedural risk and efficacy than chronologic age, and the relationship between patient weight and ablation outcomes in children with WPW remains poorly characterized.

OBJECTIVES: This study sought to determine whether low body weight is independently associated with major adverse events (MAEs) and acute ablation success in pediatric patients with WPW.

METHODS: A multicenter retrospective cohort study was performed using the IMPACT (Improving Pediatric and Adult Congenital Treatment) registry of the NCDR, including patients aged 1 to 21 years undergoing first-time EPS for WPW at 84 centers from April 2016 to December 2019. Patients with congenital heart disease, cardiomyopathy, or more than 1 ablation target were excluded. Subjects were stratified by a weight threshold of 30 kg (1 SD below the cohort mean). The primary outcome was MAEs; additional outcomes were deferred ablation, cryoablation use, and acute ablation success.

RESULTS: Among 4,456 subjects, 14% weighed <30 kg. Preprocedural supraventricular tachycardia was more common (45% vs 29%; P<0.001) and right septal pathways less common (25% vs 33%; P<0.001) in the <30 kg group. MAEs were rare but more frequent in patients <30 kg (0.3% vs 0.05%; P=0.040). Deferred ablation (9% vs 12%; P=0.070) and cryoablation use (11% vs 11%; P=0.700) did not differ. Acute success was higher in patients <30 kg (95% vs 92%; P=0.009) and persisted after adjustment (OR: 1.60; 95% CI: 1.01-2.70; P=0.046).

CONCLUSIONS: In pediatric WPW, weight <30 kg was associated with a small but elevated risk of MAEs and was an independent predictor of acute ablation success after adjustment for accessory pathway type and location, whereas deferred ablation and cryoablation use were similar across weight groups.
