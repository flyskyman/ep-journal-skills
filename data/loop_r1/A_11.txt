Takotsubo syndrome (stress cardiomyopathy) is a reversible left ventricular systolic dysfunction that presents as acute heart failure, typically following emotional or physical stress, and produces transient apical ballooning or regional wall-motion abnormality that mimics acute coronary syndrome (ACS). Episodes occurring shortly after pacemaker implantation have been reported, but recurrence within a short interval after apparent recovery is rarely described. We report a patient who underwent dual-chamber pacemaker implantation for atrioventricular block and developed acute heart failure one day after discharge. Bedside echocardiography demonstrated a regional wall-motion abnormality, and after exclusion of ACS the patient was diagnosed with previously unrecognized stress cardiomyopathy. With supportive, obstruction-aware management and continuation of renin-angiotensin system blockade, the initial episode resolved. Despite guideline-concordant therapy and structured follow-up, stress cardiomyopathy recurred three months later. This case illustrates very early, procedure-associated Takotsubo syndrome with short-interval recurrence and highlights that recurrence can occur even under guideline-concordant treatment. Takotsubo syndrome should be considered in peri-implant cardiac decompensation after pacemaker implantation; recognition in this setting is crucial and depends on timely echocardiography, exclusion of ACS, and structured follow-up.
