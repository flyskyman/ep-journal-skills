Background: Catheter ablation is an effective treatment for atrial fibrillation (AF) but is resource-intensive and not uniformly available. It is unknown how AF ablation utilization varies by geography, race, ethnicity, and socioeconomic status. Inequitable access among marginalized groups may create and perpetuate health inequities.

Objective: To determine whether ZIP-code-level racial, ethnic, and socioeconomic composition is associated with AF catheter ablation utilization.

Methods: We performed an observational utilization study using US Medicare Inpatient and Outpatient claims data files from 2016 to 2019. Medicare fee-for-service beneficiaries with a diagnosis of AF were identified, and analyses were conducted at the ZIP code level. Generalized estimating equations (GEE) were used to model the association between ZIP-code-level demographic composition and ZIP-code-level AF catheter ablation rates (patients per 10,000). Exposures were expressed per each 10% increase in a given demographic group within a ZIP code.

Results: Each 10% increase in the proportion of patients dually eligible for Medicaid (a marker of poverty) per ZIP code was associated with 275 fewer patients per 10,000 undergoing AF ablation (P=.0003). After adjustment for dual-eligible status, each 10% increase in the proportion of Black patients per ZIP code was associated with 618 fewer patients per 10,000 undergoing AF ablation (P<.0001), and each 10% increase in the proportion of Hispanic patients per ZIP code was associated with 430 fewer patients per 10,000 undergoing AF ablation (P=.002).

Conclusion: Significant inequities exist in AF catheter ablation utilization tied to racial, ethnic, and socioeconomic differences, with lower utilization concentrated in marginalized populations. These disparities have the potential to generate and perpetuate health inequities in the care of patients with AF.
