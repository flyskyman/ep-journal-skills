Background: Hydroquinidine reduces arrhythmic events in patients with Brugada syndrome (BrS), but its antiarrhythmic mechanism and its electrophysiological effect on the BrS substrate remain poorly understood.

Objective: To determine the in vivo effect of hydroquinidine on ventricular depolarization and repolarization in patients with BrS.

Methods: In this prospective in vivo human study (QUIET BrS), 12 patients with Brugada syndrome were evaluated at baseline and on treatment with hydroquinidine 300 mg twice daily. Ventricular depolarization and repolarization were characterized using standard 12-lead ECG, high-lead ECG, signal-averaged ECG, and electrocardiographic imaging (ECGI). ST-segment elevation, activation time, repolarization time, and activation-recovery interval (ARI) were computed for the ventricles and the right ventricular outflow tract (RVOT). Serum hydroquinidine levels were measured, and adverse drug events were captured by medication survey. Baseline and on-treatment values were compared in a paired analysis.

Results: On treatment, repolarization parameters over the RVOT increased significantly: repolarization time rose from 301.1 +/- 24.1 ms to 348.8 +/- 28.3 ms (P<.001), repolarization gradients from 1.1 +/- 0.4 ms/mm to 1.6 +/- 0.4 ms/mm (P<.001), and ARI from 241.3 +/- 18.1 ms to 284.8 +/- 21.5 ms (P<.001). These changes in repolarization and ARI were greater in the RVOT than in the rest of the ventricles. Activation parameters did not change significantly on treatment. ST-segment elevation over the RVOT showed a subtle increase (1.5 +/- 0.7 mV to 1.8 +/- 0.8 mV; P<.001). Serum hydroquinidine levels did not correlate with the electrophysiological changes or with adverse drug reactions. One patient developed frequent nonsustained ventricular tachycardia on treatment.

Conclusion: In patients with Brugada syndrome, hydroquinidine primarily prolongs ventricular repolarization and action potential duration, as reflected by ARI, with regional variation that is most pronounced in the RVOT, and has minimal effect on depolarization and activation. These findings localize the drug's electrophysiological action to the arrhythmogenic RVOT substrate.
