Background: Leadless pacemakers (LPMs) eliminate transvenous leads and subcutaneous pockets, thereby reducing lead- and pocket-related complications. Whether neighborhood-level socioeconomic status influences in-hospital outcomes after LPM implantation remains poorly characterized. We assessed the association between ZIP-code-based median household income and in-hospital outcomes following LPM implantation in the United States.

Methods: We performed a retrospective observational analysis of the National Inpatient Sample (2018-2022), identifying adult LPM implantations and applying discharge weights to estimate national totals. Admissions were stratified by neighborhood income quartile (Q1, lowest, to Q4, highest). Multivariable logistic regression, adjusted for demographics, comorbidities, primary payer, and hospital region, yielded adjusted odds ratios (aORs) for in-hospital mortality, pericardial complications, bleeding, transfusion, venous thromboembolism (VTE), renal complications, and device removal/revision.

Results: A total of 41,985 weighted admissions (8397 unweighted) were analyzed. Compared with Q4, Q1 patients were younger (median 77.0 vs 81.0 years; p<0.001) and had more diabetes (44.9% vs 33.8%) and end-stage renal disease (43.4% vs 36.6%). Crude in-hospital mortality was highest in Q1 (4.7%); after adjustment, Q2 had higher odds of in-hospital death than Q1 (aOR 1.517; 95% CI 1.115-2.064; p=0.008). Pericardial complications rose with income (Q1 3.2% vs Q4 4.1%), with Q4 showing higher adjusted odds versus Q1 (aOR 1.753; 95% CI 1.244-2.469; p=0.001). Q4 had lower adjusted odds of bleeding (aOR 0.788; 95% CI 0.633-0.982; p=0.034) and transfusion (aOR 0.645; 95% CI 0.494-0.842; p=0.001), whereas VTE was more likely in both Q2 and Q4 than in Q1. Median billed hospital charges were highest in Q4, while median length of stay was longer in Q1 (6.0 vs 5.0 days; p<0.001).

Conclusions: Neighborhood income shows endpoint-specific rather than uniform associations with in-hospital outcomes after LPM implantation. Lower-income neighborhoods were associated with longer stays and excess early mortality, whereas higher-income neighborhoods were associated with greater pericardial complications but less bleeding and transfusion. These patterns may inform peri-procedural counseling and institutional quality review.
