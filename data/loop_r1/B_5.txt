Aims: Current guidelines do not endorse smartphone photoplethysmography (PPG) for diagnosing atrial fibrillation (AF) without confirmatory ECG, and prior validation studies were performed supervised in healthcare settings, limiting real-world generalizability. We assessed the diagnostic performance of unsupervised, ambulatory smartphone-PPG for the detection of AF and atrial flutter (AFL) in home recordings.

Methods and results: In this prospective diagnostic validation study, unselected patients undergoing direct current cardioversion for AF or AFL performed 1-min heart rhythm recordings at home, at least twice daily for 30 days after cardioversion. The CORAI Heart Monitor PPG application on an iPhone 7 (device under test) was run simultaneously with a single-lead ECG (KardiaMobile) serving as the reference standard. Both PPG and ECG recordings were interpreted manually and independently by two experienced readers. A total of 280 patients contributed 18 005 simultaneous PPG plus ECG recordings. Recordings were of sufficient diagnostic quality more often with PPG than with ECG (96.9% vs. 95.1%; P < 0.001). With AFL recordings included, manual PPG reading against manually interpreted ECG yielded a sensitivity of 97.7%, specificity of 99.4%, and overall accuracy of 98.9%. With AFL recordings excluded, sensitivity was 99.0%, specificity 99.7%, and accuracy 99.5%.

Conclusion: A novel smartphone-PPG method, used unsupervised by patients at home, achieves accurate diagnosis of AF and AFL with very high sensitivity and specificity. These findings support its use as a standalone heart-rhythm diagnostic device after cardioversion, without the need for confirmatory ECG.
