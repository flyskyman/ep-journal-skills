=== Abstract X ===
BACKGROUND: Patient safety after atrial fibrillation (AF) catheter ablation depends on understanding the drivers of early death, yet the causes of early (<=90-day) mortality after AF ablation remain poorly characterized. We sought to determine the incidence and causes of early mortality to inform efforts to improve procedural safety.

METHODS: We performed a retrospective cohort analysis of all patients undergoing AF catheter ablation within a single health system (Mayo Clinic; Rochester, Phoenix, and Jacksonville sites) between January 1, 2013, and December 1, 2021. Early mortality was defined as death within 90 days of ablation. Cause of death was ascertained through comprehensive review of the electronic health record supplemented by outside records when available, and each death was adjudicated for its relationship to the procedure.

RESULTS: Among 6723 patients undergoing AF catheter ablation, 15 died within 90 days, yielding a 90-day all-cause mortality rate of 0.22%. Most early deaths (73.3%) had no direct relationship to the ablation procedure. The most common cause of early death was sudden death (20%), followed by peri-procedural stroke (13%), respiratory failure (13%), and atrioesophageal fistula (13%); infection, heart failure, and traumatic brain injury each accounted for 7%. Mortality directly attributable to AF ablation procedural complications occurred in 4 patients, for a procedure-related 90-day mortality rate of 0.06%.

CONCLUSIONS: Early mortality after AF catheter ablation is low (0.22% at 90 days), and mortality directly attributable to procedural complications is very low (0.06%). Most early deaths were not directly related to a procedural complication, and sudden death was the leading cause. Further study is needed to define the causes and risk factors for sudden death in this population.

BACKGROUND: Catheter ablation is an established treatment for atrial fibrillation (AF), and its overall safety profile is favorable. However, the specific causes of early (≤90-day) mortality after AF ablation remain poorly characterized, leaving a gap in our understanding of what actually drives death in the peri-ablation period and how much is attributable to the procedure itself.

METHODS: We performed a retrospective cohort analysis of all patients undergoing AF catheter ablation across a single integrated health system (Mayo Clinic; Rochester, Phoenix, and Jacksonville sites) between January 1, 2013, and December 1, 2021. Early mortality was defined as death within 90 days of ablation. For each death, the cause and its relationship to the procedure were adjudicated by comprehensive review of the electronic health record supplemented by outside records when available.

RESULTS: Among 6723 patients undergoing AF catheter ablation, 15 died within 90 days, yielding a 90-day all-cause mortality of 0.22%. Of these 15 deaths, 11 (73.3%) had no direct relationship to the ablation procedure, and only 4 were directly attributable to procedural complications, corresponding to a procedure-related 90-day mortality of 0.06%. The single most common cause of early death was sudden death (20%), followed by peri-procedural stroke (13%), respiratory failure (13%), and atrioesophageal fistula (13%); infection, heart failure, and traumatic brain injury each accounted for 7%.

CONCLUSIONS: Early mortality after AF catheter ablation is low (90-day all-cause mortality 0.22%), and mortality directly attributable to the procedure is very low (0.06%), with most early deaths unrelated to a procedural complication. Sudden death was the leading cause of early death in this population, identifying it as a priority for further investigation into its causes and risk factors to further improve patient safety after AF ablation.
