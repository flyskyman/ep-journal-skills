=== Abstract X ===
BACKGROUND: miR1 (microRNA-1), encoded by the miR1-1 and miR1-2 genes, is the most abundant cardiac microRNA and is essential for normal cardiac development and electrophysiology. Hearts from patients with atrial fibrillation or acute myocardial infarction show >75% reduction in miR1 expression. However, whether miR1 deficiency is a primary etiological driver of cardiac remodeling or merely a downstream consequence of disease has remained unresolved.

METHODS: To test causality, we crossbred miR1-1 and miR1-2 knockout mice to generate a graded-deficiency model (75%KD; genotypes miR1-1+/-:miR1-2-/- and miR1-1-/-:miR1-2+/-), reducing total cardiac miR1 to approximately 25% of wild-type (WT) levels and thereby recapitulating the deficit observed in patients. Cardiac structure, function, and electrophysiology were assessed using echocardiography, ECG, patch clamp, and optical mapping, and molecular mechanisms were interrogated by RNA sequencing and proteomics. To determine reversibility, 75%KD mice received bi-weekly intravenous tail-vein injections of miR1 mimics over 6 weeks.

RESULTS: Compared with WT, 75%KD hearts developed overt remodeling, with reduced ejection fraction and fractional shortening, prolonged QRS and QT intervals on ECG, and high susceptibility to arrhythmias. At the cellular level, 75%KD cardiomyocytes exhibited prolonged action potentials with impaired repolarization and impaired excitation-contraction coupling, accompanied by reduced Na+ current, reduced transient outward K+ current, and elevated L-type Ca2+ current relative to WT. Transcriptomic and proteomic profiling showed coordinated negative regulation of cardiac muscle contraction and ion channel activity together with aberrant positive enrichment of smooth muscle contraction genes, reflecting broad dysregulation of a gene network extending well beyond direct miR1 targets. Restoration of miR1 by mimic injection significantly improved ejection fraction and fractional shortening; however, treated hearts remained susceptible to arrhythmias.

CONCLUSIONS: Graded miR1 deficiency is sufficient to induce cardiac remodeling, identifying miR1 loss as a primary etiological factor that disrupts cardiac regulatory homeostasis rather than a passive marker of disease. The incomplete rescue, in which contractile function recovered but arrhythmic susceptibility persisted, indicates a narrow therapeutic window and underscores that achieving stable, appropriately calibrated microRNA levels will be critical for effective miRNA-based therapy in cardiovascular disease.
