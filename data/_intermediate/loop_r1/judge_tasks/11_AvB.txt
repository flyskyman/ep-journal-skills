=== Abstract X ===
Background: Takotsubo (stress) cardiomyopathy is a reversible left ventricular systolic dysfunction that presents as acute heart failure, is typically triggered by emotional or physical stress, manifests as transient apical ballooning or regional wall-motion abnormality, and mimics acute coronary syndrome (ACS). Episodes occurring shortly after pacemaker implantation have been described, but recurrence within a few months of apparent recovery is rarely reported.

Case: A patient with atrioventricular block underwent dual-chamber pacemaker implantation. One day after discharge, the patient developed acute heart failure and was diagnosed with previously undiagnosed stress (Takotsubo) cardiomyopathy. The diagnosis rested on early bedside echocardiography demonstrating transient regional wall-motion abnormality together with exclusion of ACS by selective coronary evaluation. Management was supportive, with obstruction-aware care and continuation of renin-angiotensin system blockade after discharge. The initial episode resolved with recovery of left ventricular function. Three months later, despite guideline-concordant therapy and structured follow-up, the stress cardiomyopathy recurred.

Conclusion: This case illustrates a very early, procedure-associated episode of Takotsubo syndrome after pacemaker implantation, followed by short-interval (3-month) recurrence even under guideline-concordant therapy. Takotsubo syndrome should be considered in any patient with peri-implant cardiac decompensation following pacemaker implantation. Recognition in the peri-implant setting is crucial, and timely echocardiography, exclusion of ACS, and structured follow-up are central to diagnosis and care. Clinicians should remain aware that recurrence can occur despite appropriate treatment.

Episodes occurring shortly after pacemaker implantation have been reported, but recurrence within a short interval after apparent recovery is rarely described. Bedside echocardiography demonstrated a regional wall-motion abnormality, and after exclusion of ACS the patient was diagnosed with previously unrecognized stress cardiomyopathy. With supportive, obstruction-aware management and continuation of renin-angiotensin system blockade, the initial episode resolved. Despite guideline-concordant therapy and structured follow-up, stress cardiomyopathy recurred three months later. This case illustrates very early, procedure-associated Takotsubo syndrome with short-interval recurrence and highlights that recurrence can occur even under guideline-concordant treatment. Takotsubo syndrome should be considered in peri-implant cardiac decompensation after pacemaker implantation; recognition in this setting is crucial and depends on timely echocardiography, exclusion of ACS, and structured follow-up.
