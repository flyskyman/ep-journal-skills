BACKGROUND: Cardioneuroablation (CNA) is an interventional technique targeting ganglionated plexi (GPs) to modulate autonomic tone in functional atrioventricular (AV) block and cardioinhibitory vasovagal syncope. This study evaluates fractionated area mapping and peak frequency area mapping in localizing autonomic targets and regional variations in peak frequency distributions across predefined cardiac segments in patients undergoing CNA.
METHODS: A retrospective analysis was conducted on 10 patients with cardioinhibitory vasovagal syncope (n = 7) and functional AV block (n = 3). High-density electroanatomical mapping (EAM) assessed fractionated areas and peak frequency (> 550 Hz) areas across 22 predefined cardiac regions.
RESULTS: The median fractionated area was 0.8 cm2 (0.3-1.6 cm2), while the median area for regions with peak frequency > 550 Hz was 0.6 cm2 (0.3-1.0 cm2, p = 0.152). The median peak frequency at the left superior GP, where asystole or bradycardia responses occurred, was 623.0 Hz (582.0-668.0 Hz), while in regions with tachycardia responses, it was 602.0 Hz (582.0-668.0 Hz, p = 0.738). Ablation sites overlapped both mapping areas in seven patients, while two had sites limited to peak frequency areas. Peak frequency values were highest in the septal and posterior atrial walls, superior vena cava, and GP regions, indicating heightened autonomic activity.
CONCLUSION: Fractionated area and peak frequency (> 550 Hz) mapping provide complementary insights into CNA targets. Regional peak frequency variations highlight autonomic differences, supporting multi-mapping integration for procedural precision and improved outcomes.

=== Abstract Y ===
BACKGROUND: Cardioneuroablation (CNA) modulates cardiac autonomic tone by targeting ganglionated plexi (GPs) in patients with functional atrioventricular (AV) block and cardioinhibitory vasovagal syncope. However, the optimal mapping strategy to localize GP and autonomic targets remains undefined: it is unclear how fractionated-area mapping compares with peak-frequency-area mapping, and how peak frequency varies regionally across cardiac segments. We sought to compare these two high-density mapping approaches and to characterize the regional distribution of peak frequency during CNA.

METHODS: In this single-center retrospective analysis, we studied 10 patients undergoing CNA (cardioinhibitory vasovagal syncope, n=7; functional AV block, n=3). High-density electroanatomical mapping was performed during the procedure, and two mapping approaches were compared head-to-head: fractionated-area mapping versus peak-frequency-area mapping (threshold >550 Hz). Both were assessed across 22 predefined cardiac regions, and ablation-site overlap between the two maps was evaluated.

RESULTS: The median fractionated area was 0.8 cm2 (IQR 0.3-1.6 cm2) and the median area of regions with peak frequency >550 Hz was 0.6 cm2 (IQR 0.3-1.0 cm2), with no significant difference between the two (p=0.152). The median peak frequency at the left superior GP, where asystole/bradycardia responses occurred, was 623.0 Hz (IQR 582.0-668.0 Hz), versus 602.0 Hz (IQR 582.0-668.0 Hz) in regions with tachycardia responses (p=0.738). Ablation sites overlapped both mapping areas in 7 of 10 patients, while in 2 patients ablation sites were limited to peak-frequency areas only. Peak-frequency values were highest in the septal and posterior atrial walls, the superior vena cava, and the GP regions, indicating heightened autonomic activity in those zones.

CONCLUSION: Fractionated-area mapping and peak-frequency (>550 Hz) mapping provide complementary rather than interchangeable information on CNA targets, and regional peak-frequency variation reflects underlying autonomic heterogeneity. These exploratory findings support integrating multiple mapping modalities to improve procedural precision during CNA.
