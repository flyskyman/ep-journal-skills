=== Abstract X ===
Background: Patients with persistent atrial fibrillation (AF) undergoing catheter ablation are generally considered to face worse outcomes than those with paroxysmal AF, owing to greater comorbidity burden and more advanced arrhythmia substrate. Whether persistent AF independently drives worse long-term outcomes after ablation, or whether observed differences reflect coexisting comorbidities, remains unclear.

Methods: We conducted a population-based retrospective cohort study using administrative data from Ontario, Canada, including all patients undergoing de novo AF catheter ablation between April 2012 and March 2022. Persistent and paroxysmal AF patients were compared using inverse probability of treatment weighting (IPTW) to create balanced cohorts, and Cox proportional hazards models were used to estimate the effect of persistent versus paroxysmal AF. The primary outcome was a composite of all-cause mortality and all-cause hospitalization at 30 days, 1 year, and 3 years.

Results: Among 10,788 patients undergoing de novo AF ablation, 25% had persistent AF and 75% had paroxysmal AF. After weighting, the cohorts were well balanced with respect to age (standardized difference 0.027), female sex (0.018), and Charlson comorbidity score (median 0.5% in both groups; standardized difference 0.018). The composite outcome occurred at similar rates in paroxysmal versus persistent AF patients: 5.5% versus 6.3% at 30 days (hazard ratio [HR] 1.15, 95% CI 0.94-1.40, P=0.168), 19.8% versus 19.7% at 1 year (HR 1.00, 95% CI 0.90-1.11, P=0.971), and 34.1% versus 35.4% at 3 years (HR 1.05, 95% CI 0.97-1.13, P=0.269). No increased risk was observed for the individual components of all-cause mortality or all-cause hospitalization at any time point.

Conclusion: In this population-based cohort, the long-term risk of all-cause mortality and hospitalization after catheter ablation was similar for patients with persistent and paroxysmal AF through 3 years of follow-up. The adverse prognostic impact historically attributed to persistent AF appears to be driven primarily by comorbid conditions rather than by the AF type itself, suggesting that persistent AF alone should not be a barrier to ablation access.

=== Abstract Y ===
AIMS
Catheter ablation is an established rhythm-control therapy for atrial fibrillation (AF). Persistent AF is commonly presumed to confer worse long-term outcomes than paroxysmal AF because it accompanies greater comorbidity burden and a more advanced arrhythmic substrate, and concern over poorer prognosis may limit ablation access. Whether AF type itself, independent of comorbidity, drives worse outcomes after ablation remains uncertain. We sought to determine whether persistent versus paroxysmal AF independently predicts adverse long-term outcomes following de novo catheter ablation.

METHODS AND RESULTS
We conducted a population-based retrospective cohort study using administrative health data from Ontario, Canada, including 10,788 patients undergoing de novo AF catheter ablation between April 2012 and March 2022 (persistent AF, 25%; paroxysmal AF, 75%). Inverse probability of treatment weighting balanced the groups, achieving close concordance in age (standardized difference 0.027), female sex (0.018), and Charlson comorbidity score (0.018). Cox proportional hazards models estimated the effect of persistent versus paroxysmal AF on the primary composite of all-cause mortality and all-cause hospitalization. In the weighted cohort, composite event rates were comparable for paroxysmal versus persistent AF at 30 days (5.5% vs 6.3%; HR 1.15, 95% CI 0.94-1.40, P=0.168), 1 year (19.8% vs 19.7%; HR 1.00, 95% CI 0.90-1.11, P=0.971), and 3 years (34.1% vs 35.4%; HR 1.05, 95% CI 0.97-1.13, P=0.269). Neither all-cause mortality nor hospitalization differed individually at any time point.

CONCLUSION
Long-term mortality and hospitalization after AF ablation were similar for persistent and paroxysmal AF. The adverse prognosis attributed to persistent AF appears driven by comorbid conditions rather than AF type itself.
