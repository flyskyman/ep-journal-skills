BACKGROUND: In high-risk patients, ventricular tachycardia (VT) frequently persists despite maximally tolerated antiarrhythmic drugs and extensive catheter ablation. Cardiac stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT) is an emerging noninvasive treatment for refractory VT, but safety and effectiveness data, particularly at 1 year, remain limited.

OBJECTIVE: To evaluate the safety and effectiveness of cardiac SBRT through 12 months in patients with VT refractory to standard therapy.

METHODS: In a prospective single-arm registry, patients were enrolled after failure of maximal medical therapy and catheter ablation. Each patient served as his or her own control, with outcomes compared to the 6 months preceding treatment. The planning target volume was defined by integrating available electrophysiologic mapping and imaging data, and a single 25-Gy fraction was delivered with respiratory motion mitigation. VT burden (implantable cardioverter-defibrillator [ICD] shocks plus antitachycardia pacing [ATP] sequences) and clinical and safety outcomes were assessed at 6 weeks, 6 months, and 12 months.

RESULTS: Fifteen patients were enrolled and underwent planning; 14 (93%) received treatment. Twelve (80%) survived to 6 weeks and 10 (67%) to 12 months; 4 deaths occurred after treatment. Treated VT was reduced by 98% from 6 weeks to 6 months and by 99% at 12 months versus the 6 months before treatment. Recurrent VT (appropriate ATP or ICD shock) occurred in 33% (4 of 12) between 6 weeks and 12 months. A nonsignificant trend toward lower amiodarone dose was observed at 12 months, with no change in ventricular function.

CONCLUSION: In selected high-risk patients with VT refractory to standard therapy, cardiac SBRT was associated with marked reduction in VT and appropriate ICD therapies over 1 year.

=== Abstract Y ===
Background: Refractory ventricular tachycardia (VT) persists in high-risk patients despite maximal antiarrhythmic drug therapy and extensive catheter ablation. Cardiac stereotactic body radiotherapy (SBRT) has emerged as a noninvasive treatment option, but safety and effectiveness data, particularly at 1 year, remain limited.

Objective: To evaluate the safety and effectiveness of cardiac SBRT for refractory VT over 12 months of follow-up.

Methods: We conducted a prospective, single-arm registry enrolling patients with VT refractory to maximal medical and catheter ablation therapy. Target volumes were defined by integrating available electrophysiologic mapping and imaging data, and patients received a single 25 Gy fraction with respiratory motion mitigation strategies. Each patient served as their own control, with outcomes compared to the 6-month period preceding treatment. VT burden was defined as the combination of implantable cardioverter-defibrillator (ICD) shocks and antitachycardia pacing (ATP) sequences. Clinical, arrhythmic, and safety outcomes were assessed at 6 weeks, 6 months, and 12 months.

Results: Of 15 patients enrolled and undergoing treatment planning, 14 (93%) received SBRT. Twelve patients (80%) survived to the end of the 6-week period, and 10 (67%) survived to 12 months; 4 deaths occurred after treatment. Treated VT was markedly reduced compared with the 6 months before treatment, with a 98% reduction from 6 weeks to 6 months and a 99% reduction at 12 months. VT recurrence (appropriate ATP or ICD shocks) between 6 weeks and 12 months occurred in 33% of patients (4 of 12). A nonsignificant trend toward lower amiodarone dosing was observed at 12 months. No changes in ventricular function were detected.

Conclusion: In a select group of high-risk patients with VT refractory to standard therapy, cardiac SBRT was associated with a substantial reduction in VT burden and appropriate ICD therapies sustained over 1 year. These findings support SBRT as a promising noninvasive option warranting further investigation.
