=== Abstract X ===
BACKGROUND: Pulsed field ablation (PFA) is increasingly used for pulmonary vein isolation in atrial fibrillation. Intravascular hemolysis has recently been recognized as a side effect of PFA, raising concerns about renal safety. However, whether PFA-induced hemolysis carries broader systemic thromboinflammatory consequences, and how PFA's overall thromboinflammatory potential compares with that of radiofrequency ablation (RFA), remains unknown.

OBJECTIVE: To assess the thromboinflammatory potential of PFA relative to RFA and to characterize the systemic biological effects of PFA-induced hemolysis.

METHODS: In a prospective comparative study, 60 patients with paroxysmal atrial fibrillation undergoing pulmonary vein isolation were treated with a pentaspline PFA system (n=30) or RFA (n=30). Biomarkers of inflammation, platelet activation, endothelial cell damage, hemolysis, and renal function were measured at 3 periprocedural timepoints. Pericarditis, early recurrences, and 6-month rhythm outcomes were also assessed.

RESULTS: Intravascular hemolysis occurred in 100% of PFA patients after a mean of 42.8 ± 3.7 deliveries. Leukocyte release was similar between PFA and RFA (delta 3.0 ± 2.3 vs 3.6 ± 2.3 ×10^9/L; P=.254), as were acute changes in platelet activation and endothelial cell damage biomarkers. Nitric oxide decreased significantly only in the PFA group (−26.5% ± 17.8%; P<.001). No acute kidney injury occurred; however, hemolysis burden was significantly associated with creatinine increase, inflammation, and platelet activation. Female sex was the only independent predictor of significant hemolysis after PFA (OR 7.8; 95% CI 1.2–50.2; P=.031). In-hospital pericarditis was lower with PFA than RFA (10.0% vs 33.3%; P=.028). Early recurrence (20.0% vs 26.7%) and late recurrence (6.7% vs 10.0%) were similar between groups (all P>.05).

CONCLUSION: PFA produces a thromboinflammatory reaction comparable to RFA. PFA-induced hemolysis occurs in all patients even with a limited number of deliveries and is significantly associated with renal function decline, inflammation, and platelet activation, with women at higher risk of significant hemolysis. These findings support monitoring hemolysis burden after PFA, particularly in female patients, even when overt acute kidney injury is absent.

BACKGROUND: Hemolysis was recently identified as a side effect of pulsed field ablation (PFA), which raised renal safety concerns. Further systemic effects of PFA-induced hemolysis remain unknown.
OBJECTIVE: This study aimed to investigate the thromboinflammatory potential of PFA and the systemic biological effects of PFA-induced hemolysis.
METHODS: We prospectively included 60 patients with paroxysmal atrial fibrillation receiving pulmonary vein isolation using a pentaspline PFA system (n = 30) or radiofrequency ablation (RFA) (n = 30). Biomarkers of inflammation, platelet activation, endothelial cell damage, hemolysis, and renal function were analyzed at 3 timepoints. Pericarditis, early recurrences, and 6-month rhythm outcomes were further assessed.
RESULTS: Intravascular hemolysis was detected in 100% of PFA-treated patients after a mean of 42.8 ± 3.7 deliveries. Leukocyte release (Δ3.0 ± 2.3 vs 3.6 ± 2.3 × 109/L; P = .254), acute platelet activation, and endothelial cell damage biomarker changes were similar between PFA and RFA. Nitric oxide levels decreased significantly in the PFA group only (-26.5% ± 17.8%; P < .001). Although no acute kidney injury occurred, hemolysis burden was significantly associated with creatinine increase, inflammation, and platelet activation. Female sex was the only independent predictor of significant hemolysis after PFA (odds ratio, 7.8; 95% confidence interval, 1.2-50.2; P = .031). In-hospital pericarditis was lower with PFA (10.0% vs 33.3%; P = .028), whereas early (20.0% vs 26.7%) and late recurrences (6.7% vs 10.0%; all P > .05) were similar between groups.
CONCLUSION: PFA is associated with a similar thromboinflammatory reaction as RFA. PFA-induced hemolysis occurs in all patients and is significantly associated with renal function decline, inflammation, and platelet activation even with limited PFA deliveries. Females are at a higher risk of significant hemolysis after PFA.
