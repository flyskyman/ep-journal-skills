BACKGROUND: Cryoballoon (CB)-guided pulmonary vein isolation (PVI) is an established treatment for atrial fibrillation (AF). This observational study aimed to assess the role of individual anatomical characteristics to predict long-term freedom from arrhythmia recurrence after CB-guided PVI for paroxysmal AF (PAF).
METHODS: Three hundred fifty three consecutive patients (58 ± 11 years, 56% males), undergoing PVI between 2012 and 2018 were analysed. Individual pulmonary vein (PV) anatomy was assessed using preprocedural cardiac magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). For each PV, the cross-sectional area (CSA) was calculated. The impact of PV characteristics and CSA on long-term AF-free survival was evaluated.
RESULTS: Acute PVI was achieved in all patients. Two hundred twenty-three patients (63%) had a normal PV anatomy (2 left- and 2 right-sided PV). Variant PV anatomy was present in 130 patients (37%). During the observation period of 48 months, AF-recurrence was documented in 167 patients (47 %). Patients with AF-recurrence presented with significantly enlarged right-sided PVs and left superior PVs (LSPVs) (p < 0.001). The presence of left common PVs (LCPVs) (n = 75, Log-rank p < 0.001) as well as right variant PVs (n = 35, Log rank p < 0.001) was associated with a significantly impaired long-term AF-free survival rate as compared to patients with normal PV characteristics.
CONCLUSION: Variant PV anatomy is a good predictor for AF-recurrence. A correlation between an enlarged CSA of right-sided PVs as well as LSPVs and AF-recurrence was documented.

=== Abstract Y ===
Background: Cryoballoon (CB) pulmonary vein isolation (PVI) is an established single-shot strategy for paroxysmal atrial fibrillation (AF), yet arrhythmia recurrence remains common. Whether individual pulmonary vein (PV) anatomy and ostial dimensions predict long-term arrhythmia-free survival after CB-guided PVI is unclear.

Objective: To determine whether anatomical PV characteristics predict long-term freedom from AF recurrence after single-shot CB-guided PVI.

Methods: We performed a single-center observational cohort study of 353 consecutive patients (mean age 58 +/- 11 years; 56% male) undergoing CB-guided PVI for paroxysmal AF between 2012 and 2018. Preprocedural cardiac MRI was used to characterize individual PV anatomy and to compute the cross-sectional area (CSA) of each PV. Patients were stratified by anatomical subgroup (normal vs. variant anatomy, including left common PV [LCPV] and right variant PVs) and by enlarged vs. normal PV CSA. Long-term AF-free survival was compared between subgroups by log-rank analysis over a 48-month observation period.

Results: Acute PVI was achieved in 100% of patients. Normal PV anatomy (two left and two right PVs) was present in 223 patients (63%) and variant anatomy in 130 patients (37%). Over 48 months of follow-up, AF recurred in 167 patients (47%). Compared with patients who remained arrhythmia-free, those with recurrence had significantly enlarged right-sided PVs and left superior PVs (LSPVs) (p < 0.001). An LCPV was present in 75 patients and was associated with significantly impaired long-term AF-free survival compared with normal anatomy (log-rank p < 0.001), as were right variant PVs (n = 35; log-rank p < 0.001).

Conclusion: In patients undergoing single-shot CB-guided PVI for paroxysmal AF, variant PV anatomy was a strong predictor of AF recurrence, and enlarged CSA of the right-sided PVs and LSPVs correlated with recurrence. Preprocedural assessment of individual PV anatomy may help identify patients at higher risk of recurrence and inform candidate selection for CB-guided PVI.
