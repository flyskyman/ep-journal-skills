BACKGROUND: Selection of radiofrequency ablation (RF) or cryoablation (Cryo) for atrioventricular nodal re-entrant tachycardia (AVNRT) in children remains controversial due to a lack of contemporary comparison studies in this population.
OBJECTIVES: This study sought to compare outcomes of RF and Cryo for AVNRT in the pediatric population.
METHODS: AVNRT ablation outcomes were retrospectively analyzed utilizing the National Cardiovascular Data Registry IMPACT (Improving Pediatric and Adult Congenital Treatment) Registry from April 2016 to March 2019. Data from subjects 1 to 21 years of age undergoing elective first-time slow pathway (SP) modification for AVNRT were included. Exclusion criteria included <1 year of age, congenital heart disease, and >1 ablation target. Cases were analyzed by ablation energy: 1) RF only; 2) Cryo only; 3) radiofrequency ablation switching to cryoablation (RF→Cryo); and 4) cryoablation switching to radiofrequency ablation (Cryo→RF). The primary outcome was acute ablation failure. Secondary outcomes included in-hospital adverse events.
RESULTS: Among 2,448 patients (mean age 13.6 ± 3.4 years, 60% female), RF only was employed in 43% (n = 1,046), Cryo only in 49% (n = 1,201), RF→Cryo in 6% (n = 135), and Cryo→RF in 66 (3%). Acute ablation failure occurred in 1.3% (n = 33), with no difference by energy source (1% in RF only, 1.5% in Cryo only, 1.5% in RF→Cryo, 3% in Cryo→RF; P = 0.5). Atrioventricular (AV) block requiring permanent pacemaker did not occur in any group; transient AV block occurred in 0.4% of the cohort, with no difference by group.
CONCLUSIONS: In this largest pediatric study of AVNRT ablation, RF and Cryo demonstrated comparable high acute success and rare documentation of AV block that did not result in temporary or permanent pacing. Longitudinal data are important for further comparison of these modalities with regard to recurrence risk and late complications.

=== Abstract Y ===
BACKGROUND: Slow pathway modification is the standard treatment for atrioventricular nodal reentrant tachycardia (AVNRT), and both radiofrequency (RF) and cryoablation (Cryo) energy are used. However, the choice between energy sources in children remains unsettled, as no contemporary head-to-head comparison exists in the pediatric population.

OBJECTIVES: This study compared acute ablation failure and atrioventricular (AV) block requiring pacing between RF and Cryo for first-time slow pathway modification in children.

METHODS: A retrospective cohort analysis was performed using the National Cardiovascular Data Registry IMPACT (Improving Pediatric and Adult Congenital Treatment) Registry from April 2016 to March 2019. Patients 1 to 21 years of age undergoing elective first-time slow pathway modification for AVNRT were included; those younger than 1 year, with congenital heart disease, or with more than 1 ablation target were excluded. Cases were grouped by energy strategy: RF only, Cryo only, RF switching to Cryo (RF->Cryo), and Cryo switching to RF (Cryo->RF). The primary outcome was acute ablation failure; secondary outcomes were in-hospital adverse events.

RESULTS: Among 2,448 patients (mean age 13.6 +/- 3.4 years; 60% female)—the largest pediatric AVNRT ablation cohort reported to date—43% (n = 1,046) underwent RF only, 49% (n = 1,201) Cryo only, 6% (n = 135) RF->Cryo, and 3% (n = 66) Cryo->RF. Acute ablation failure occurred in 1.3% (n = 33) overall and did not differ by energy source (RF only 1%, Cryo only 1.5%, RF->Cryo 1.5%, Cryo->RF 3%; P = 0.5). No patient in any group developed AV block requiring permanent pacing (0%). Transient AV block occurred in 0.4% of the cohort, with no difference between groups.

CONCLUSIONS: In this large registry-based pediatric cohort, RF and Cryo achieved comparable, high acute success for AVNRT slow pathway modification, and AV block was rare and did not result in temporary or permanent pacing. Energy source did not influence acute outcomes, supporting either modality for initial pediatric AVNRT ablation; longitudinal follow-up is needed to compare recurrence and late complications between modalities.
