BACKGROUND: Pneumopericardium is a rare complication of epicardial ablation after dry pericardiocentesis to treat ventricular arrhythmia (VA); its exact clinical effects on patients are still unclear. The purpose of this study was to evaluate the clinical effects of pneumopericardium during epicardial ablation on patients with VA.
METHODS: Patients with VA who underwent epicardial catheter ablation under local anesthesia at West China Hospital of Sichuan University from August 2012 to January 2022 were enrolled in this study. The incidence of pneumopericardium was investigated. The occurrence of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACEs) was evaluated 1 year after the operation.
RESULTS: A total of 86 VA patients were included in the study. Twenty-two cases had pneumopericardium, with an incidence rate of 25.6%, and 12 (54.55%) patients complained of dyspnea during the procedure with an average occurrence time of 5.4 ± 3.2 min after pericardiocentesis. The blood pressure (BP) decreased significantly, with the mean BP dropping from 119.8/73.2 to 103.5/64.9 mmHg (p < 0.001). None of the cases progressed to tension pneumopericardium. Postoperative follow-up with a median period of 411 days showed that the incidence rate of major adverse cardiovascular events (MACEs), including the composite endpoints of all-cause death, rehospitalization for heart failure, and tachyarrhythmia events, was 36.4% (n = 8) in the pneumopericardium group and 35.5% (n = 23) in the non-pneumopericardium group. The Kaplan-Meier survival analysis showed that there was no statistically significant difference in the incidence of MACEs between the two groups (p = 0.28).
CONCLUSIONS: The incidence of pneumopericardium during epicardial ablation was relatively high. However, if recognized early and managed properly, it is unlikely to progress to tension pneumopericardium. The occurrence of pneumopericardium during the procedure may not significantly affect the long-term prognosis of patients.

=== Abstract Y ===
BACKGROUND: Epicardial catheter ablation following dry pericardiocentesis is an established approach for refractory ventricular arrhythmias (VAs). Pneumopericardium is a recognized but rare complication of this access route; however, its clinical consequences—both intraprocedurally and on long-term outcome—remain poorly defined, leaving operators without guidance on whether its occurrence warrants altered management or signals worse prognosis.

OBJECTIVE: To evaluate the clinical effects of intraprocedural pneumopericardium during epicardial catheter ablation in patients with VAs.

METHODS: This was a single-center observational cohort study at West China Hospital of Sichuan University. Consecutive VA patients undergoing epicardial catheter ablation between August 2012 and January 2022 were enrolled; all procedures were performed under local anesthesia. Patients were classified into a pneumopericardium group and a non-pneumopericardium group and compared for intraprocedural events and long-term outcomes. The primary long-term endpoint was major adverse cardiovascular events (MACE), a composite of all-cause death, rehospitalization for heart failure, and tachyarrhythmia events. MACE-free survival was compared between groups by Kaplan-Meier analysis.

RESULTS: Among 86 patients, pneumopericardium occurred in 22 (incidence 25.6%). Of these, 12 (54.55%) reported dyspnea during the procedure, occurring a mean of 5.4 ± 3.2 min after pericardiocentesis. Onset was accompanied by a significant fall in blood pressure, from 119.8/73.2 mmHg to 103.5/64.9 mmHg (p < 0.001). No case progressed to tension pneumopericardium. Over a median follow-up of 411 days, MACE occurred in 8 patients (36.4%) in the pneumopericardium group versus 23 (35.5%) in the non-pneumopericardium group, with no significant difference in MACE incidence between groups by Kaplan-Meier analysis (p = 0.28).

CONCLUSIONS: In this single-center cohort, pneumopericardium was a relatively common finding during epicardial ablation in VA patients, typically heralded by transient dyspnea and a modest blood-pressure decline shortly after pericardiocentesis. When recognized early and managed appropriately, it did not progress to tension pneumopericardium and was not associated with worse long-term prognosis. These findings suggest that intraprocedural pneumopericardium, while warranting prompt recognition, need not by itself prompt aggressive intervention or raise concern for adverse outcome.
